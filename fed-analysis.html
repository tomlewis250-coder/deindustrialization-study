National Manufacturing Employment

The long decline of American manufacturing employment, with key periods and cumulative job losses.

5.3 Million Jobs Lost Since Peak

U.S. manufacturing employment peaked at 17.9 million workers in February 1990. Today, only 12.6 million remain—a decline of nearly 30%. The shaded areas show recession periods, with the Great Recession (2007-2009) causing the steepest single decline.

The China Shock (2000-2010)

The decade following China's WTO entry was devastating for American manufacturing. Employment fell 18.2% between 2000 and 2007—before the Great Recession even began. Combined with the recession, approximately 5.8 million manufacturing jobs disappeared in a single decade. Research shows regions most exposed to Chinese imports experienced higher unemployment, lower labor force participation, and increased disability claims.

Employment by Period

Period Change Driver
1990-2000 -1.4% Early automation, NAFTA
2000-2007 -18.2% China WTO, offshoring
2007-2010 -16.8% Great Recession
2010-2019 +7.2% Partial recovery
2020-Now +3.1% Post-COVID reshoring

Key Insight

Manufacturing job losses during recessions have become permanent. Unlike previous economic cycles where manufacturing employment recovered, post-2000 recessions resulted in structural job losses that never returned.

Each recovery since 2000 has reached a lower employment peak than the previous one, suggesting the decline is structural rather than cyclical.

Rust Belt State Analysis

The industrial heartland has borne the brunt of manufacturing decline.

1.5 Million Rust Belt Jobs Lost Since 2000

Seven Rust Belt states—Pennsylvania, Ohio, Michigan, Indiana, Illinois, Wisconsin, and West Virginia—have collectively lost over 1.5 million manufacturing jobs since 2000. West Virginia (-45.4%) and Pennsylvania (-41.8%) experienced the steepest declines.

State-by-State Impact

Pennsylvania: Lost 403,000 manufacturing jobs. Once a steel powerhouse, now struggles with rural decline. Susquehanna County lost 53% of manufacturing jobs.

West Virginia: Worst percentage decline at -45.4%. Combined with coal collapse, the state has few remaining industrial employers.

Illinois: Lost 358,000 jobs (-38.7%). Chicago-area manufacturing devastated; Caterpillar and heavy equipment declines.

Ohio: Lost 351,000 jobs (-33.7%). Cleveland, Toledo, Youngstown hit hard. Some recovery with Honda and auto parts suppliers.

Michigan: Lost 213,000 jobs (-26.3%). Auto industry restructuring; Detroit's decline symbolic of broader trends.

Indiana & Wisconsin: Fared better (-14% and -12%) due to diversified manufacturing base and auto transplants.

Philadelphia Fed Manufacturing Survey

55+ years of manufacturing sentiment data from America's oldest regional Fed survey.

The 2020s: Weakest Decade on Record

The Philadelphia Fed's General Activity Index measures whether manufacturers are expanding or contracting. Positive readings indicate expansion; negative readings indicate contraction. The 2020s have averaged -0.9, with only 48% of months showing expansion—the weakest performance in the survey's 55+ year history.

Performance by Decade

Decade Avg Index % Expanding
1970s +7.2 62%
1980s +5.8 58%
1990s +11.4 68%
2000s +3.1 51%
2010s +8.7 64%
2020s -0.9 48%

How to Read This Data

Diffusion Index: Calculated as % of firms reporting increase minus % reporting decrease. Ranges from -100 to +100.

Above 0: More firms expanding than contracting (growth)

Below 0: More firms contracting than expanding (decline)

Why it matters: Regional Fed surveys are leading indicators, released before national data. Persistent negative readings signal weak manufacturing conditions.

Capacity Utilization & Production

The paradox of rising production with falling employment.

The Productivity Paradox

One of the most striking findings: industrial production has grown 65% since 1990 even as employment has fallen 30%. This means fewer workers are producing more goods than ever before. The divergence reflects massive productivity gains through automation, robotics, and technology—not sector collapse.

What This Means

Capacity Utilization at 75.5%

  • Below historical average of 77.2%
  • Indicates slack in the system
  • Firms don't need to expand capacity
  • Limited pricing power for manufacturers
  • Weak incentive for new hiring

Employment vs. Production Divergence

  • Automation replaced routine manual work
  • Remaining jobs require higher skills
  • Capital investment substitutes for labor
  • New factories employ far fewer workers
  • Trend will accelerate with AI/robotics

Decade-by-Decade Regional Fed Performance

How manufacturing sentiment has evolved across six decades of Federal Reserve survey data.

The 2020s Are the Weakest Decade in Survey History

Aggregating all available Fed surveys by decade reveals a clear secular trend: the 1990s were the strongest decade for manufacturing sentiment, and the 2020s are the weakest. The Philadelphia Fed's 55+ year history shows the 2020s averaging -0.9 with only 48% of months in expansion—worse than even the 2000s (the China Shock decade).

Why the 2020s Are Alarming

  • Post-COVID "manufacturing boom" narrative doesn't match survey data
  • Net-negative readings despite strong consumer demand
  • All four surveyed Fed districts show contraction or near-zero
  • Only Kansas City (energy/ag equipment) shows positive readings

What Drove the 1990s Strength?

  • Strong domestic demand pre-WTO/NAFTA era
  • Export manufacturing still competitive globally
  • Tech manufacturing boom (computers, electronics)
  • Defense buildup early in decade

The Manufacturing Wage Premium

Manufacturing has historically paid above-average wages. How has that premium changed over 30+ years?

Manufacturing Still Pays More—But the Gap Has Narrowed

Manufacturing average hourly earnings have consistently exceeded all-private sector averages since 1990. However, the premium as a percentage has compressed as other sectors (particularly finance and tech) have raised wages. This matters because the replacement jobs in deindustrialized regions are primarily in sectors paying below the manufacturing wage.

The Wage Replacement Gap

Jobs Lost (Manufacturing Average)

  • Avg annual earnings: ~$60,000
  • Full benefits including pension
  • Union representation in many cases
  • Career progression within single employer

Jobs Added (Regional Replacement Avg)

  • Healthcare aide: ~$32,000
  • Retail associate: ~$31,000
  • Hospitality: ~$23,000
  • Typically fewer benefits, no pension

The Ripple Effect: True Job Loss Scale

Every manufacturing job supports multiple additional jobs through supply chain and consumer spending effects.

4.6 Million Direct Losses → ~18 Million Total Economic Impact

Using Bureau of Economic Analysis input-output multipliers, each manufacturing job supports approximately 4 additional jobs through supply chain (indirect) and consumer spending (induced) effects. The 4.6 million direct job losses since 2000 represent a total economic ecosystem impact approaching 18 million jobs—roughly equivalent to losing the entire workforce of California's metropolitan economy.

Why Multipliers Matter for Policy

When policymakers debate the cost of trade policy, they typically cite the 4.6M direct job number. But communities don't just lose factory workers—they lose the hardware stores, restaurants, contractors, and services that those workers supported. The auto industry has a multiplier of 8×: one auto assembly job supports 7 others. West Virginia's coal and mining job losses triggered exactly this kind of community-wide collapse.

Manufacturing Recovery: Who's Winning?

Analysis of which states gained manufacturing jobs 2015-2023, and the factors behind recovery.

Recovery Is Real—But Concentrated in the South

Between 2015 and 2023, a manufacturing recovery emerged in select states. Texas, Tennessee, Georgia, and the Carolinas have added manufacturing jobs while traditional Rust Belt states continued to decline. This "Sun Belt shift" reflects a structural geographic reorientation of American manufacturing, driven by right-to-work laws, lower labor costs, and auto transplant investment (BMW, Mercedes, Toyota, Volkswagen).

Gainers: Common Factors

  • Right-to-work states: Lower union costs attract investment
  • Auto transplants: BMW (SC), Toyota/Mazda (AL/TX), VW (TN)
  • Lower cost of living: Workers accept lower nominal wages
  • State incentive packages: Tax breaks for factory siting
  • Port access: SC, GA ports grew with export manufacturing

Why Rust Belt Isn't Recovering

  • Higher legacy labor costs and union density
  • Aging infrastructure vs. greenfield Southern sites
  • Brain drain as younger workers left over decades
  • Skills mismatch for new advanced manufacturing
  • Ongoing structural exit of legacy industries

The Public Health Dimension: Opioid Crisis

Deindustrialized regions show disproportionately high opioid mortality rates—a devastating public health consequence.

Strong Negative Correlation: More Job Loss = More Deaths

State-level data shows a meaningful negative correlation between manufacturing employment decline since 2000 and opioid mortality rates (CDC WONDER, 2022). West Virginia—which lost 45.4% of its manufacturing jobs—has an opioid death rate of 80.9 per 100,000, the highest in the nation and nearly 3× the states that maintained manufacturing employment. This correlation does not prove causation, but it is consistent with the "deaths of despair" research by Case and Deaton.

The "Deaths of Despair" Connection

Princeton economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton (2020) documented a rise in "deaths of despair"—suicide, drug overdose, and alcoholic liver disease—concentrated in working-class communities without college degrees. Their research found these deaths correlated with the long-term decline of stable blue-collar employment. Manufacturing job loss removes not just income but also social identity, community anchors, and purpose—risk factors for addiction and mental illness.

West Virginia: Case Study in Compounding Crisis

  • Manufacturing: -45.4% (2000-2023)
  • Coal employment: -71% (2012-2022)
  • Opioid deaths: 80.9 per 100,000 (2022)
  • Poverty rate: 17.1% (highest in nation)
  • Population loss: -3.2% since 2010

Low-Deindustrialization States: Contrast

  • Nebraska: +1.4% mfg change
  • Opioid rate: 11.2 per 100,000
  • Iowa: -6.2% mfg change
  • Opioid rate: 15.1 per 100,000
  • Poverty rates: ~10-11% (national avg)

Fed District Survey vs Economic Outcomes

Linking current manufacturing sentiment to poverty rates and long-run employment changes across districts.

Districts with Weaker Manufacturing Show Higher Poverty

Plotting the 2020s average manufacturing diffusion index for each Fed district against the average state poverty rate in that district reveals a pattern: districts with persistent negative survey readings (Philadelphia, Richmond) have higher poverty rates than districts with positive readings (Dallas, Minneapolis). This is not a smoking gun—causality flows in multiple directions—but it validates that survey sentiment tracks real economic conditions.

Key Conclusions

What the Data Shows

  • Structural decline: Jobs lost don't return after recessions
  • Geographic concentration: Rust Belt lost 40-45% of mfg jobs
  • Productivity gains: Output up while employment down
  • Weak surveys: 2020s weakest decade in 55+ years
  • Low capacity: 75.5% vs 77.2% historical average
  • ~18M total jobs: Multiplier effect means 4× more than headline
  • Opioid link: Strong correlation with mortality in hard-hit states
  • Recovery skewed South: Sun Belt gaining, Rust Belt still declining

Policy Implications

  • Job training: Workers need skills for new industries
  • Infrastructure: Modernization could attract investment
  • Education: STEM critical for advanced manufacturing
  • Regional policy: Place-based interventions needed
  • Realistic expectations: New factories employ fewer workers
  • Mental health: Opioid correlation demands public health response
  • Wage gap: Replacement jobs pay $20-30K less annually
